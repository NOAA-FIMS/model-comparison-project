Abstract

As NOAA Fisheries transitions to next-generation stock assessment methods, including the Fisheries Integrated Modeling System (FIMS), it is important to evaluate how its performance compares with established modeling tools. Using an age-structured operating model, we compared FIMS estimates with four widely used assessment models in the United States. Comparisons with ADMB models, the Age Structured Assessment Program, Stock Synthesis, and the Beaufort Assessment Model, focused on verifying estimates of key assessment quantities, including spawning biomass, recruitment, and fishing mortality. Comparisons with the Woods Hole Assessment Model focused on evaluating the performance of random-effects configurations. Results indicate that estimates from FIMS are highly consistent with those from established modeling frameworks, with median relative error closely aligned across key metrics. Differences among models were generally minor and primarily associated with differences in the sum-to-zero constraint on recruitment deviations, equilibrium initialization for initial numbers-at-age, and recruitment bias correction. Overall, these findings demonstrate that FIMS provides a reliable and modular framework for U.S. fisheries stock assessment while maintaining high standards of estimation accuracy and enabling flexible random-effects modeling.

  • A total of 500 simulation replicates were initially generated to ensure sufficient sample size for convergence analysis. The final analysis was performed on a subset of 100 simulations that successfully converged across all models.
  • Model performance metrics included:
    • Median relative error (MRE) and median absolute relative error (MARE) for key quantities such as spawning biomass (\(SB\)), recruitment (\(R\)), and fishing mortality (\(F\)).
      • Relative error (RE) was calculated as \(\frac{\hat{\theta}_i}{\theta_i} - 1\), where \(\theta_i\) is the true value for simulated dataset \(i\) and \(\hat{\theta}_i\) is the value estimated from fitting the model to the simulated data.
      • 95% confidence intervals of the MRE were calculated using the binomial distribution to estimate bias of each EM for a given OM (Thompson (1936)).
      • To summarize the bias across simulations and years for each model, we calculated the median RE across years for each simulation and then took the mean of these medians across simulations (Stock and Miller (2021)).
      • MARE across all years and simulations was calculated to quantify the overall accuracy, providing a measure of the magnitude of estimation errors.
    • Convergence rate (\(CR\)) for each estimation model (\(m\)) was calculated as \(CR_m = \frac{N_{converged, m}}{N_{total, m}}\), where \(N_{converged}\) represents simulations meeting both the gradient and Hessian criteria.

Results

  • Time-series estimates from all EMs captured the overall trends observed in the operating model, and the variability across the 100 simulations was consistent among EMs and closely matched the variability of the OM (Figure 1).
  • Across all EMs, the median RE remained close to zero, indicating minimal bias in estimates relative to the OM. The distributions of REs showed consistent patterns among EMs over time (Figure 2). In the early years, REs were generally small and narrowly distributed, reflecting high precision in model estimates. In later years, the distributions widen across all EMs, indicating increased variability in the estimates. Overall, both the central tendency and variability of REs were similar across EMs, with FIMS configurations performing comparably to the other EMs (Figure 3).
  • The mean of median RE for FIMS showed strong consistency with other EMs. Across all metrics, FIMS values were within the same decimal range as the others. In addition, random-effects models consistently reduced bias compared to their fixed-effects counterparts across all models.
  • Summary statistics of absolute relative error across all years and simulations showed similar performance among EMs for all quantities of interest (Table 2). For fishing mortality, FIMS performed comparably to the other models, with a lowest MARE of 2.63%. Recruitment estimates exhibited the largest errors across all models. However, the FIMS random-effects model produced the lowest MARE (3.82%), slightly outperforming ASAP, BAM, SS3, and WHAM (3.83%–3.87%). For spawning biomass, FIMS yielded lowest MARE values (2.08% and 2.11%), indicating high estimation accuracy relative to the OM. Overall, the error metrics for FIMS were similar to those of the established models across all quantities, demonstrating that FIMS achieved comparable estimation performance.
  • Convergence rates varied across EMs, with TMB models generally exhibiting higher values than ADMB models under the specified convergence criteria (Table 3). Both FIMS and WHAM showed particularly strong convergence when configured with random effects. However, these results are based on a one-size-fits-all criterion and may change if model-specific gradient thresholds are applied.

Discussion

  • FIMS produced estimates of spawning biomass, recruitment, and fishing mortality that were highly consistent with those from established stock assessment models, indicating comparable estimation accuracy across metrics of interest.
  • The similarity in performance across models suggests that, when configured equivalently, differences in underlying software frameworks (e.g., ADMB vs. TMB) have minimal impact on estimation outcomes. This consistency is particularly important for ongoing transitions in assessment platforms, as it indicates that adopting FIMS is unlikely to introduce systematic differences in key management quantities.
  • Small differences among models likely resulted from implementation details such as recruitment deviation constraints (e.g., sum-to-zero assumptions) and approaches to equilibrium initialization of numbers-at-age (working in progress).
